Using Magic Powers

Magic in The Tower Builders is about manipulating the occult nature of an object to change things, create new situations, see through time, or even just blast your enemies. Fortunately, manipulating the invisible forces that pervade reality is rarely straightforward. You read that correctly: if it were straightforward it wouldn’t be any fun. As you read the following, keep in mind there are definitely ways to play in the Tower Builders setting that keep your creativity centered on character, story, and problem solving. If you’re interested in that sort of play we’d suggest using Abilities with the usual SFX rules, or see ways of using the setting. Still, magic in the Tower Builders setting should have in-character meaning. There should be a theory of magic that makes magic in the setting more than just wishing for anything that could happen.

The following rules are an attempt to expose some of the artistry of magic to the player. When you use magic in the game, you should feel a connection between what the rules require and what the character must do, so that everyone at the table knows why your character is waving their hands around like that. The following describes how to use magic powers, for creating those powers see magic powers.

Introduction

When players are building characters, they will want to know what their character is capable of, and how to build a character with the abilities they envision. So in a game without clearly defined spells, what can a character do with magic? The answer comes from the theory of magic as manipulating the hidden occult nature of things as described in fundamentals of magic. Story-wise, magic is always doing one of two things:

  1. Taking the occult nature that already exists in the target and shifting it around, OR
  2. Adding occult nature to the target from somewhere or something else, OR
  3. Some combination of both.

Definitions

Power traits that represent magic in the Tower Builders Setting have some common features. The following terms are helpful to create a common language among the players.

Result
The intended purpose of any use of magic. After casting, the system result of the effect of a successful spell such as an asset or complication.
Magical Power
A Power or Ability that follows the rules of magic for the setting.
Magical Power Set
A Power Set that collects a set of magical powers and abilities thall are all a use of the same core feature, training, or magical item.
Taxonomies Set
A power set that collects all of the powers and specialties that represent a character’s known taxonomies.
Taxonomy Power
A power that represents a particular view and description of the physical, magical, or symbolic components of the world. A taxonomy comprises a set of elements it treats as fundamental and can be used to magically change a target that matches the element or give a property of one of those elements to a target.
Taxonomy
A description of the elements of the world and how that interact both naturally and unnaturally. Alternate terms are Language, Pantheon, Alphabet, and Ontology.
Reference
A feature of the world a taxonomy can influence or create, but not necessarily fundamental to the taxonomy, or available as an element trait. For example, the Taxonomy Forest Life will be able to create results on or related to snails, even though snails are not a fundamental part of how the taxonomies describe forests.
Element
1. A specialty of a taxonomy power that is included in dice pools when creating a result or affecting a target described by the element. 2. A major component of a Taxonomy that defines and creates the taxonomy’s worldview. Many alternate names exist including: Glyph, Deity, Symbol, Rune, Word.
Target
The subject of a spell. This can be another spell. See [targeting].
Casting
The specific activity(s) a character takes to create a magical result.
Casting Implement
A wand, staff, amulet, or other physical object that assists in casting. Usually a signature asset.
Spellcaster
Spellcaster, Spellworker, Wizard, Magus and many other terms refer to a character trained to cast some form of flexible magic. In the standard form of magic in The Tower Builders they will have at least a few elements or a taxonomy for such purposes. Any character may have magic Powers or Power Sets, but they are not trained to use them in a flexible way like a spellcaster.
Magic Dice
Any dice in a pool that come from a Magical Power Set or a taxonomies power set, and sometimes a magical distinction die.
Casting Dice
Any dice in a pool that represent the mundane activities a character is undertaking to create a magical result. Usually dice from the two prime sets: attributes and training.
Targeting Method
The method a caster is using to deliver magic to a target. Usually one of Touch, Bolt, Focus, or Affinity.

Basics

The Basics of Material Magic and Material Components

As an example, let’s return to an acorn. An acorn could one day be a tree. Because of the Law of the Path, the acorn’s occult nature includes that future possible tree. So a Magician that wants to drop that acorn on the ground and have it grow instantly into a tree bring must the future occult nature of the acorn into its current nature. That’s an example of manipulating the acorn’s occult nature. A character might do that in one of many ways, such as by a using magical power built from the Time Control ability or a power built around controlling nature or trees.

Now imagine you want to make an acorn into a magic acorn, that makes a living target freeze in place, as if turned to stone. Nowhere in the occult nature of an acorn is the ability to turn objects into stone. But acorns can last a long time before they are planted and grow into a tree. Can we treat acorns as having an occult nature that includes hibernation or suspended life? Certainly! Broad definitions like this can create disagreement about when an object has a certain occult nature, and when it does not. As a way to manage these challenges, if the GM or group decides that a use of an item’s occult nature is valid, but on the edge of what’s valid, you should increase the difficulty of any result that uses a weak occult nature of that item. This is necessarily a judgement call and each group will find its favorite ways of making that call. Some groups will let the GM make the call alone, but others may put it up to a vote by the table. However you would like to do it is up to your group.

So, back to magic acorns that turn people into stone. So an acorn might be able to force someone into hibernation, but that’s a far cry from stone. 80’s fantasy movies aside, what then is a good option for creating a stone inducing throwable weapon? Pebbles are an obvious one, but even better would be a stone that was once a living thing or at least strongly related to a living thing. Consider:

  • Petrified wood.
  • Kidney or bladder stones, or any of the various stones that come from a living thing.
  • Bones.
  • Seashells.

All of the above are items that were once living, but are now stonelike. They are each very well suited for magic that turns living things to stone. But we wanted magic not to turn someone into stone, but to create an item that, when thrown at a living target, turns it to stone. Let’s put a pause on that and come back to it in magic items.

The Basics of Taxonomies and Elements

In the above, we considered doing magic by including an object as a source of the occult nature required for the spell. That is called material magic or using material components in magic. If you read the fiction sprinkled through the book, you will notice plenty of examples of magicians that do not seem to use material magic at all. These mages are always waving their hands and speaking in mysterious languages opaque to the outsider. The may draw symbols in the air, or call to gods they have relationships with. They are doing the same thing as above, but instead of getting the occult nature they need from an item, they are creating it from their mind. Characters that use magic like this are using a Taxonomy. With enough study and practice, the magician can rely directly on the symbols they have carved into their own minds to be powerful enough to create the occult nature they need. This is Symbolic Magic, or using symbolic components in magic. A collection or structured group of symbols is called, generally, a taxonomy. Magicians of most cultures have discovered this kind of magic, so different traditions will use these techniques in a wide array of ways. Taxonomies will look different from culture to culture, and some cultures may have a single taxonomy that they equate with fundamental truth.

As an example, wizards from farming cultures will have a Taxonomy constructed of symbols for working animals, vital crops, seasons and family structures. Those from peoples with strong philosophical traditions will have abstract ideas within their Taxonomies, such as chaos, the atom, sets of virtues, and so on. Different magic school will teach different Taxonomies and most will favor one or two over others, so the taxonomies a particular character uses will relate to their identity and history.

Consider our above example of turning someone to stone. Think through the legends and stories you know, are there any that feature as a core theme restriction of movement, action, or even literally turning someone into stone? I’ll imagine there are. The Greek legend of Pygmalion is about an artist that so fell in love with his creation that it came to life. A wizard might have a Taxonomy called Greek Myth, within which a symbol called Pygmalion is available for use. So can a wizard use Pygmalion to create acorns that turn enemies to stone? Certainly! They simply need to infuse the acorn’s occult nature with the occult nature from their deep study of greek myth and the Pygmalion story. But it’s important to remember that when the Pygmalion element is summoned you get all of it at once. The caster must manage the parts of the element they do not want. Complications that appear when hitches occur might involve awkward emotional relationships with the target of the magic!

Casting

Magic always changes or manipulates the occult nature of an object in a way that makes it different than another non-magical object of its type. This can happen for many reasons, and we encourage GMs and Players to play around with including naturally magical things in their games. But for the most part, the PC’s in Tower Builders will be spellworkers, wizards, witches, mages, warlocks and so on. And spellworkers cast. The following are some rules and suggestions for how the metaphysics of magic in Tower Builders should limit when Magical abilities and elements apply to your roll.

What Casting Is

Casting refers to the activities that a character undertakes to create a magical Result. Casting is what you do to make magic happen. In many cases, this will match what we see from popular fiction about magic: shouting in magic languages, gesturing with hands and magic wands, boiling up disgusting brews and so on. But magical trait dice are dice like any others, and so should be available for inclusion in pools even when the character is not initiating a spell. For instance, when they are defending against someone else’s spell it may not be clear that they are casting a spell of their own. To clarify: Casting is the activities you undertake to make magic happen. This means that everything from the use of imagination and meditation, to carving symbols into stone, to undertaking a grand quest to save the queen can be casting. It is the wizard’s intent (and years of magical study) that make it a magical act. However, not just any activity is well suited for casting, the clever wizard will always select the casting methods that hit the sweet spot between ease, detail, time, cost, practicality, symbolism, and everything else.

The core of this is the Limit requires casting. Any Power set that requires casting, should be unavailable when the character (not necessarily the player) can’t find a way to meet their own power’s casting needs. Rather that write a long description of what’s required, characters should consider the description of their Magic Power distinction to be the core of this. This is where they decribe how their magic works in the broadest sense. To complicate matters, you may sometimes feel like casting should be a different action than using the result. But there are a few universally applicable guidelines that everyone should use when considering what activities to choose for casting.

  1. Effort Matters. The bigger and more grand the result, the bigger and more grand the casting methods should be. This is reflected in SFX that allow the character to take on extra effort (more time, fancier materials) to step up or double dice. There are also SFX that step down dice when everything does not quite align. But it’s also a general truth of magic, and can guide the GM in determining the base difficulty for casting. See [Magical Style SFX]
  2. Symbolism matters. The better the activity aligns with the intended result the easier the spell.

Along with the Requires Casting Limit, every magic power requires targeting. Targeting is explained in another section, but the method a character uses usually sets some requirements for the roll. In system terms, it determines what dice you are using for casting and therefore what complications apply to the roll. These "casting dice" are usually one attribute and one training trait.

When casting a spell, if the player is using a Magical Power for a result that matches its description, the power works as inended without explanation. If the character is using a taxonomy power the player should describe to the table what elements of the occult nature of the target you are changing, and have a source of any necessary occult nature that is not already present. The player should also describe what the character is doing to create the magical result. This does not always need to be detailed or unique, many powers will always work the same way, but it can be a lot of fun when improvising.

Example Casting

Jerry’s character Isabel wants to give her loyal malamute, Rex, wings and increase his size so that she can use him to fly over Aurilia’s castle walls. Being a docg, Rex has no connection to any creature that can fly and so no occult nature helpful for the Result. This is how the conversation at the table might go:

Jerry: So, I want to give Rex wings and make him big enough to ride.

GM: Ok, how are you going to do that?

Jerry: Well, Isabel has her Bag of Many Components which could have an feather of a large flying creature, and she has studied the taxonomy Nobility of Pastoral Life, which should definitely have a Reference for beasts of burden in it.

GM: Ok, great. This will be a test, and the base difficulty is two d8’s. So you have an elements that could help your with flight or a riding animal?

Jerry: No, the only specialties I have are 'Oxen' and 'Self Sufficiency'. Does oxen count?

GM: Oxen would help him carry heavy weight, but being Oxen being large heavy animals, I think you’d cause yourself more trouble than that element is worth since you want to fly.

Jerry: Right, I won’t include it then.

GM: Ok, I’m going to up the difficulty to d10’s because I think a feather from a large enough bird might be pretty hard to keep from getting crushed in that bag. You get your casting dice, your 'Bag of Many Components' die, whatever distinctions apply, and your Pastoral Life die. How is Isabella casting this?

Jerry: The usual improvisational rhyming, and I’ll stick the feathers to his shoulder blades with some tree resin.

GM: Ok, so your casting dice are Voice and Perform.

Jerry: All right, so I have: The Villiage Witch distinction die, Hands and Perform for casting, Bag of Many Components signature asset, and Nobility of Pastoral Life.

GM: Rolling difficulty dice . . .

As you can see above, the player needed to determine what Abilities and Taxonomy(s) they were using, and what the character was doing in the fiction to cast the spell. These actions determined what dice were available for the roll.

Building a Dice Pool

Like anything in Cortex, the character’s goal determines the dice they can recruit for their dice pool. When a character is casting magic and has abilities and power sets as described in magic powers, they can gather dice in the following way:

  1. Description: Describe the intended result.
    • The result and target must allow you to include at least one magic die, whether it’s from your character sheet or an available asset. If you cannot find a magic die that could create the intended result and act on the target, your magic cannot help you.
  2. Casting Dice: Select a Targeting method appropriate to the ability and circumstances and choose an Attribute and Ability as Casting Dice.
    • If circumstances restrict a character from using the Traits recommended in Targeting they may be able to choose other casting dice, but they may also consider switching targeting methods. Any limit that applies should be honored.
    • Complications and stress on these traits apply as usual.
  3. Magic Dice: The intended result or the target determine the distinction, magical ability, taxonomy and element dice that may be included.
    • Distinctions: Characters will usually have a Distinction that describes their magic. This is the most obvious Distinction to use, but mundane distinctions may be more appropriate especially when Hinder-ing their efforts.
    • Magical Abilities: If a magical ability’s description could create the result you may include it. Note that descriptions often prescribe an element that must be present in the result or target when using that Ability. See the SFX Modify Ability for a way around this.
    • Taxonomies: If the elements of a taxonomy apply to the target or result, include the taxonomy that most closely applies. If the taxonomy’s elements apply weakly, make the roll harder (choose a consistent method such as stepping down the die or improving the opposition's pool).
    • Elements: If an element die of a taxonomy applies to the target or result, include it as well.

Targeting

When casting, a character must choose how the magic reaches the target of the magic. Below are the most common targeting methods and recommendations about what casting dice they imply the character should use. There will always be exceptions and good ideas should be supported as long as they meet the requirements of the fiction. Note that many magical items act automatically or are always on, and usually use touch as their targeting method.

  • Touch - Available when the caster is in contact with, within, or otherwise directly connected to the target. If the intended scale of the Result is large enough forests, cities, lakes etc. may count as a touched target as much as a small location can. When choosing casting dice, choose the atrribute and training combination that best suits the actions the character is taking. Some advice:
    • Craft for creating magical items.
    • Focus for quiet concentration.
    • Influence when magically changing a character’s mind.
    • Know when using sensory powers that require knowledge to interpret, such as Divination or Precognition.
    • Labor when the spell or ritual is an act of work with significant duration.
    • Move when the act of moving creates the magical result.
    • Notice when using sensory results to pick out details in a scene.
    • Operate for using complex magical items that require understanding of how to use them.
    • Perform for large rituals that require keeping the attention of an audience.
    • Sneak when being unseen is necessary to create the magic. Unwilling targets may require Hands and/or Fight instead of other Attribute or Traiting dice to keep in contact long enough for the spell to be cast.
  • Bolt - The spell will be cast as a bolt, beam of energy or other carrier that is sent to the target from the caster’s hands, wand, staff or other magical implement. Test or contest pools may include mundane defenses such as armor or dodging since the bolt must physically fly to the target. Pools usually include hands and fight, but if the target is willing they may use other Attributes or skills.
  • Focus - The spell is cast upon a target that is visible, audible, or can otherwise be sensed with any sense that allows attention to be directed. The caster must direct their gaze or attention on the sensation of the target, and any complications that might distract or obstruct the sense in question apply. Dice Pools usually include eyes and focus.
  • Affinity - This targeting technique allows a caster to cast a spell on a target that they cannot directly sense. Casting spells on a remote target feels appropriately magical, but if it is too easy it can seriously derail plotlines. The GM should choose one or more of the following options depending upon how difficult they want this technique to be in their campaign:
    1. Require creation of a connection asset before casting the spell. This connection asset should go both ways, so that attacking characters of similar skills with the affinity method is dangerous.
      1. Creating this asset should require a deep familiarity with the target, or an item meaningful to or once a part of the target. Collecting such items could be a great source of intrigue in a campaign that features espionage or paranoia. So you could create a connection asset to your childhood best friend easily, but would struggle to connect to the shopkeep you bought flour from last week. What counts as an appropriate item should be dependent upon the setting and technology. In some settings an image of the target may suffice, but the utility of imagery may be mystically devalued in a modern world of ubiquitous photography.
      2. Items used as above might be rated with a die size, which could limit the maximum effect die of the connection asset.
      3. The connection asset may be a bottleneck for the magic. In this case the connection asset may:
        1. Limit the maximum effect die to the connection asset die size.
        2. Limit the size of or replace the casting dice or magic dice.
    2. Affinity casting could require an SFX, consider the example below:
      1. SFX- Affinity Casting: Spend a pp to cast a spell on a remote target that you have an element of or significant relationship to.
    3. Make Affinity Casting impossible or a lost or secret technique known only to a few, or even declare it a technique that hasn’t been invented yet.
